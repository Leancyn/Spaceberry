Modul 1: Principal Strategies in Short Conversations

A. Objectives

After studying this material, students are able to:

  • Put more focus on the last line
  • Find out the similarity in meaning (synonym) between the questions and answer choices
  • Be familiar with the words of similar sounds which may distract or trick them when answering

B. Material Description

1.a. Focus on the Last Line

In short conversations, it's important to focus on the last line—the utterances spoken by the second speaker. These lines are often related to both the question and the answer.

Example:

Woman: Do you know where Dean Nicholson’s office is?
Man: There’s a directory in the entrance way.
Narrator: What does the man imply about Dean Nicholson’s office?

Answer: C. He doesn’t know where the office is.

1.b. Answer with Synonyms

Sometimes, answers use different words that mean the same as those in the questions. Understanding synonyms is essential.

Example:

Woman: How’s your research project coming, Mike?
Man: So far, so good.
Narrator: What does Mike mean?

Answer: D. Until at the moment, he has not had any problems.

Exercise 1.2

Do the same with the next set of dialogues. Focus on the use of synonyms.

1.c. Avoid Homophones

Homophones are words that sound similar but have different meanings. They can be used to confuse listeners, so learn to identify and eliminate them.

Example:

Man: I’d like coffee with milk, please.
Woman: Would you like a large or a small mug?
Narrator: What did the woman ask?

Answer: D. what size mug the man wants

Exercise 1.3

Write down the conversations and underline the homophones in the answer choices.
